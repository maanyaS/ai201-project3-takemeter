## Community: r/csMajors
I chose csMajors because there has been a rise of anxiety over the current job market amongst CS students and experienced professionals. Due to this, the community receives a lot of regular posts seeking advice to stand out in the job market, expressing anxiety over their future, or expressing their opinions and hot takes regarding the future of programming and software engineering. This community is therefore a good fit for a classification task because of the repetitive patterns followed by the posts. By classifying the posts in this subreddit, I will be able to analyze the differences in the posts better and understand the regular patterns between posts. This will benefit the community as it helps them better understand the recent trends in people's attitudes towards CS in the subreddit.

## Hard edge cases: 
A post that combines a mix of two of the labels or even three will be ambiguous to differentiate. Some posts express hot take opinions, reactions to the hot takes or a recent event, and then advice on how to not be a victim of the event or hot take, all in a single post. I will handle this by observing what the main focus of the post is. If the post focuses more on receiving advice, then I will categorize it as advice_seeking. If a post focuses more on opinions regarding the situation, I will label it opinion. If the post is complaining about the situation more, I will label it rant.

The difference between rant and opinion can be slim at times as well. When people rant, they generalize negative statements about the world, which is their opinion. However, in these situations, it is important to note whether the text was written emotionally or rationally. Opinions are usually talked about rationally with no surgence of emotions or anger that could distort the writer's thinking. However rants, are more emotional, which cause extreme opinions to be talked about within the rant. These will still be categorized as rants, as the text was emotional and shows the writer's anger and hopelessness.

Another hard edge case is that the difference between advice_seeking and question might be too slim. A lot of posts asking for advice are questions. This could confuse the model. I will make sure to reduce confusion in the model by labeling any question asking for tips to improve personal circumstances as advice. Any question that is general and not meant for improving oneself will be labeled as question.

For any post that feels like a combination of 2 labels, I will ask myself, what is the main focus of the post? Which label received majority number of words? This is how I will categorize edge cases.

I will collect my examples from posts and comments under the r/csMajors community. If a label is underrepresented after 300 examples, I will update my search to reflect the kind of post that I am looking for. This will help me find the more niche posts that fall under my underrepresented label.

## Evaluation metrics: Which metrics will you use to evaluate your model and why are those the right ones for this specific task? (Accuracy alone is not enough — explain what else you need and why.)
I will evaluate if the model is accurate or not. I will do this by using a:
1. Per-class F1 Score- This will balance the precision and recall for each label individually, so I will be able to see if the model is doing well on one label but failing on the other.
2. Macro-averaged F1- This is the average F1 across all 4 labels, and helps treat each equally regardless of how common the label is. This penalizes the model for ignoring rare labels.
3. Confusion matrix- This will show which labels are getting mixed up (for example, if advice_seeking is getting mixed up with question). This will help me determine if the model is able to effectively differentiate between the edge cases.

## Definition of success: What performance would make this classifier genuinely useful? What would you accept as "good enough" for deployment in a real community tool?
My model will be successful if it can successfully help the Reddit community understand the discours in the r/csMajors subreddit. This can be identified if:
1. Macro F1 ≥ 0.70 as a minimum threshold for deployment. This means the model is meaningfully better than random guessing across all labels. A score of 0.70 means that on average, the model is correctly identifying and capturing the right posts for each label about 70% of the time, balancing both false positives and false negatives. For a community analysis tool like this, a 0.70 threshold is appropriate because the goal is to identify broad discourse trends rather than make high-stakes decisions, so occasional misclassifications are tolerable as long as the overall patterns remain meaningful.
2. F1 ≥ 0.65 on my hardest labels (question vs advice_seeking). F1 ≥ 0.65 on the hardest labels specifically targets question and advice_seeking since I already identified these as my most ambiguous boundary in my edge cases section. A slightly lower threshold of 0.65 is set for these labels because their similarity in structure — both are phrased as questions — makes them inherently harder to distinguish, and some level of confusion between them is expected even for a human annotator. Tracking these labels separately ensures I don't overlook a situation where the model is performing well overall but consistently failing on the one boundary that matters most to my classification scheme.

3. Confusion between adjacent labels should be low — mixing up rant and opinion is more forgivable than mixing up rant and question, since those are conceptually further apart.
